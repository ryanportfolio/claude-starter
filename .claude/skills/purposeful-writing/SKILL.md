# Purposeful Writing

## Overview

Write for the reader's next decision.

Every piece of writing has a job: answer a question, earn trust, explain a choice, persuade, request action, record a decision, or make an idea clear.

Find that job. Do it early. Remove everything that does not help.

Optimize for:

- directness
- relevance
- specificity
- clarity
- natural voice
- useful structure

Do not write to sound impressive. Write to make the reader understand, believe, remember, or act.

## When to Use

Use for any drafting or revision task, including:

- application answers
- emails
- essays
- bios
- cover letters
- proposals
- memos
- reports
- product copy
- explanations
- messages
- speeches
- long-form prose
- de-slopping a draft that reads as machine-made
- matching a writer's own voice

Use a more specialized skill when the task requires domain-specific rules, such as research, legal writing, technical documentation, or book chapters.

## Before Writing

Identify, silently:

1. **Reader:** Who will read this?
2. **Purpose:** What should they understand, feel, decide, or do?
3. **Prompt:** What is the question actually asking?
4. **Constraints:** Length, format, tone, required details, and deadline.
5. **Best material:** Which facts, examples, experiences, or reasons carry the answer?

If the writer provides a sample of their own writing, read it first and mirror its rhythm, word choice, and punctuation habits. Do not upgrade a casual voice into corporate prose.

Then write the answer. Do not narrate this planning process.

## Core Rules

### Answer early

Lead with the answer, point, request, or most useful fact.

Do not make the reader wait through background, disclaimers, or scene-setting unless the task truly needs them.

### Be specific

Use concrete details instead of claims about yourself or the subject.

Weak:
> I am a strong leader who works well with others.

Better:
> I ran the weekly handoff between design and engineering, which cut the average review cycle from five days to two.

Do not invent details, outcomes, credentials, or experiences. Invented specificity is worse than vague prose. If the material is thin, write plainly from what is known.

### Match the form to the job

Do not force every task into the same shape.

- **Application answer:** Answer the prompt, give one relevant example, show what it reveals.
- **Email:** State the purpose, give essential context, make the request or next step obvious.
- **Explanation:** Start with the answer, then explain how or why.
- **Proposal:** Name the problem, recommend the move, show why it is worth doing.
- **Memo:** State the decision or finding, then the implications and action.
- **Bio:** Lead with the most relevant identity or work, then evidence of it.
- **Essay:** Lead with tension or a claim worth following, then earn it.
- **Product copy:** Lead with the user's task or outcome, not the product's self-description.

### Use plain, active language

Prefer:

- concrete nouns
- strong verbs
- short words when they say the same thing
- active voice when it clarifies responsibility

Avoid:

- jargon
- inflated claims
- corporate filler
- generic AI phrasing
- empty intensifiers
- obvious transitions
- unnecessary throat-clearing

Say "I led the project," not "I was responsible for leading the project."

Do not trade one tell for another. Replacing em dashes with a pile of semicolons or parentheses is the same problem in new clothes.

### Cut AI tells

Certain words and shapes mark writing as machine-made. Scan for them, then cut or rewrite:

- **Inflated vocabulary:** delve, crucial, pivotal, testament, showcase, foster, leverage, utilize, robust, seamless, vibrant, tapestry, underscore, landscape (abstract). Use the plain word.
- **Copula dodges:** "serves as," "stands as," "boasts." Say "is" or "has."
- **Fake structure:** rule-of-three lists ("innovation, inspiration, and insight"), "not just X, it's Y," and "from X to Y" ranges where X and Y are not a real scale.
- **Filler openers and closers:** "Great question," "Let's dive in," "It's important to note," "I hope this helps," "In today's world."
- **Formula sections:** "Despite these challenges, X continues to thrive," "The future looks bright."
- **Style tics:** em dashes, colons used as mid-sentence connectors, boldface on every noun, inline-header bullets that restate their own label, Title Case headings, decorative emojis.
- **Vague attribution:** "experts say," "studies show," "it is widely regarded." Name the source or cut the claim.
- **Feeling over mechanism:** "stays close at hand," "unlocks seamless experiences." Say what it does, returns, or costs.

For before/after examples of any tell, see the `humanizer` skill's `patterns.md`.

### Preserve voice and add soul

Make the writing sound like a thoughtful person, not a template.

- Keep distinctive phrasing when it is clear. Preserve earned confidence, dry humor, directness, and intentional roughness.
- Have a view. React to the facts; do not only list neutral pros and cons.
- Vary rhythm. Short sentences. Then a longer one with room to turn. Uniform sentence length reads as machine output.
- Let some asymmetry in. Perfect parallel structure feels generated.
- Use "I" when it fits. First person is not unprofessional.

Fix accidental awkwardness. Do not smooth the writing into bland professional language.

### Earn every claim

Do not say:

- passionate
- innovative
- hard-working
- strategic
- collaborative
- transformative

unless the surrounding sentence shows what that looks like.

Demonstration beats self-description.

### Respect constraints

Meet word limits, requested formats, and required questions exactly.

A short response should not contain a long introduction. A formal response should not become stiff. A personal response should not become confessional unless the prompt calls for it.

## Revision Pass

Before delivering, check:

1. **Prompt:** Did this answer the actual question?
2. **Purpose:** Is the intended takeaway clear?
3. **Order:** Does the strongest, most useful material arrive early?
4. **Specificity:** Are claims supported by concrete detail?
5. **Voice:** Does it sound human and appropriate to the writer?
6. **Economy:** Can any sentence be cut or shortened without loss?
7. **Reader:** Is the next action, decision, or takeaway obvious?
8. **Constraints:** Does it fit the requested length, format, and tone?
9. **Self-audit:** Read it once more. What still reads as a template or a bot? Fix that, then stop.

Never use em dashes.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Writing around the answer.**
   State the answer first.

2. **Using adjectives in place of evidence.**
   Show the action, choice, result, or observation.

3. **Sounding like a template.**
   Replace generic phrases with the writer's real language and details.

4. **Over-explaining.**
   Give the reader what they need, then stop.

5. **Mistaking formality for stiffness.**
   Be professional without becoming impersonal.

6. **Mistaking personality for disclosure.**
   Be concrete and human without sharing irrelevant private detail.

7. **Ignoring the prompt's hidden demand.**
   A question about challenge may really ask for judgment, resilience, growth, or fit. Answer that, not merely the chronology.

8. **Ending weakly.**
   End on the decision, result, implication, request, or memorable point. Do not trail into summary.

9. **Fabricating specifics to sound human.**
   Invented numbers, quotes, or credentials are worse than plain honest prose.

10. **Trading one tell for another.**
    Do not swap em dashes for semicolon piles, or one filler phrase for another.
